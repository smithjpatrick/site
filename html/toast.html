Redesigning a self-ordering restaurant kiosk to drive restaurant adoption by addressing their pain points and those of their patrons.

Client overview

Toast, Inc. is a producer of point-of-sale hardware and software systems for restaurants. They want to increase the adoption of their self-order kiosks in restaurants to capitalize on the growing demand for kiosk technology in the food service industry.

Toast enlisted the help of my team and me to understand the needs of both restaurants and patrons with respect to kiosk usage and deliver a solution to address them.

Our solution

After talking with restaurant patrons and managers, as well as looking at Toast's competitors, my team determined that a successful self-order kiosk should address the following areas:

  • Fast ordering

    People told us they expect to save time using a kiosk. We heard them and provided functionality to get them in and out quickly with their food.

  • Personalization

    We added a level of personalization so patrons can rely on past orders to inform current decisions.

  • Customization

    We built in robust menu item modifications so patrons can get exactly what they want without asking any questions.

  • Upselling

    We incorporated upselling into the order flow to increase average check sizes and restaurant revenue.

Our process

We began the work by learning from kiosk stakeholders (patrons, restaurant managers) and competitors. This research informed our exploration of potential solutions, which we tested with participants and later refined for presentation to the client.

Our Solution

After an initial client meeting and interviews with a restaurant manager and patrons, my team learned that a successful design needed to support the goals of not just the end user, but also the actual customer of the kiosk itself - the restaurant owner or manager. To that end, our design focused on delivering improvements in the following areas:

  • Time-savings. One of the primary reasons one may use a kiosk to order is to skip a long line and save themselves time, so our kiosk helps patrons get in and out more quickly.
  • Personalization. A preferred method of ordering is using one’s own mobile device. It’s more personal and allows users to act upon set preferences. We wanted to take advantage of purchasing history from repeat customers to provide a level of personalization on a public device.
  • Item modification. One of the top requests from restaurants is improved menu modifications, to cater to patron needs and also help generate revenue for restaurants.
  • Upselling. Kiosks have a higher potential to increase average check sizes, so we wanted to implement upselling opportunities that were appropriate and did not feel overly obtrusive.
